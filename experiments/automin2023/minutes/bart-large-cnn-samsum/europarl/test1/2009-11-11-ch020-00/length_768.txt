SUMMARY
- The European Parliament supports the abolition of the visa regime for all the countries of the Western Balkans.
- Slovenia's initiative allows its citizens to start travelling freely to the Schengen area from 19 December 2010.
- Macedonia, Serbia and Montenegro should be allowed to join it as soon as possible.
- PERSON232: Bosnia and Herzegovina and Albania need to be prepared for visa abolition.
- The European Union has a political responsibility to finalise the process of visa liberalisation and tomorrow Parliament's broad support is expected in this regard.
- The Council Presidency was not invited and was told there was no need for it to be here.
- The Commission welcomes the adoption of the legislative proposal by both the European Parliament and the Council on short-stay visa policy.
- The Commission will monitor closely the fulfilment of all the criteria laid down in the road map.
- The former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia had already been the subject of a positive assessment and the dialogue was concluded with that country.
- Serbia and Montenegro hosted missions led by the Commission, in association with the experts from the Member States, and those missions confirmed that all of the criteria of the respective road maps had been fulfilled properly.
- Bosnia and Albania have not yet been able to complete the necessary reforms requested in road map to justify the exemption of the visa requirement.
- Parliament will be a Visa-free travel to Western Balkans is a far-sighted and hard-headed move.
- It will promote security and promote understanding and tolerance.
- The aim of the declaration is visa liberalisation for all Western Balkans citizens as soon as possible.
- The rapporteur, Mrs Fajon, has been working hard for the last two months.
- The PPE Group is committed to speeding up visa liberalisation for all countries in the Western Balkans by July 2010.
- It took them three months of negotiations to convince the political Left in this House to operate according to the treaties.
- The responsibility for any delays rests with Albania and Bosnia and Herzegovina.
- PERSON239 thanks the rapporteurs, Mrs Fajon and Mrs Ludford, on behalf of the Committee on Foreign Affairs, Committee on Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs, and the shadow rapporteur.
- They have succeeded in presenting a proposal demanding that the Commission and the Council come up with an overall package for all the people of the entire Western Balkans.
- They hope that the goal of visa liberalisation should also apply to Kosovo and that it will be achieved for Bosnia and Albania by the middle of next year.
- Tomorrow the European Parliament will vote on abolishing the short-stay visa requirement for Balkan countries.
- Bosnia and Herzegovina and Albania are not eligible to receive the visa waiver on 1 January.
- The European Parliament needs to send a signal to them to meet the criteria.
- The abolition of the visa requirement for travel to the European Union has political and technical significance for the citizens of the Western Balkans.
- The majority of the population of the former Yugoslavia has been denied the opportunity of travelling freely across Europe for nearly 20 years.
- The Macedonians, Montenegrins, Serbs, Bosnians, Herzegovinians, Albanians and Kosovars should join the EU as early as possible in 2010.
- PERSON245 believes that we are on the right track with regard to the Balkan States.
- The citizens of Western Balkan countries will be able to move freely around the European Union.
- The European football championships will be held in Poland and Ukraine in 2012, so the possibility of free movement will be important.
- PERSON250 congratulates rapporteur and shadow rapporteur for drafting an acceptable compromise on visa liberalisation for Bosnia and Herzegovina, Albania and Kosovo.
- Hungarian MEP congratulates the Joint Declaration and report.
- The debate is closed.
- The vote is tomorrow at 11.00.

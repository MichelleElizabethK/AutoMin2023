SUMMARY
- The European Network and Information Security Agency (ENISA) was founded in 2004.
- Its task is to improve network and information security in the European Union and to promote closer cooperation among the Member States.
- Parliament and the Council decided before the summer recess that ENISA's mandate should be extended for three years.
- Giles Chichester and Angelika Niebler are asking the Commission about the future of ENISA.
- ENISA's mandate is extended for a further three years.
- Commission will prepare a questionnaire to be submitted to public online consultation on the possible objectives of a modernised network and information policy at EU level in the second half of 2008.
- The Commission will consult with ENISA and its management board.
- The European Parliament and Council have approved the extension of ENISA's operation until the end of 2012.
- The Greek government will cover the maintenance expenses of an ENISA office in Athens to facilitate the body's work and operations.
- The majority of partners agree that ENISA is the most able and qualified body to develop a new European policy for the security of networks and information.
- The Council and the European Parliament have agreed to the extension of ENISA's operating regulation until 2012.
- The European Commission's proposal to establish a European authority for the purchase of electronic communications has been treated with circumspection.
- The recent attacks on Estonian cyberspace and that of other countries show how vital it is to come up with a convincing and coordinated European response immediately.
- The security of networks is influencing more and more aspects of our lives and the growth of our economy.
- Estonia, Georgia and the U.S. became dependent on network-driven economy.
- The Commission recognised the need for a network-security agency.
- ENISA was evaluated after only a year and its existence was called into question.
- There is unanimous support in the Council and in the European Parliament for extending the duration of ENISA for three years.
- Both arms of the legislature agreed to reach a first-reading agreement as soon as possible.
- The Council plans to adopt the amending regulation as an 'A' point at a forthcoming Council meeting.
- The problem would then be resolved and Parliament can start a real debate on the future of cyber-attacks.

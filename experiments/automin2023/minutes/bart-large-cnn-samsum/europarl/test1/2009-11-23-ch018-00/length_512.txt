SUMMARY
- The next item is the joint debate on the following reports: on the draft Council Decision adopting the rules on the confidentiality of Europol information and on the list of third States and organisations with which Europol shall conclude agreements.
- The initiative of the Kingdom of Belgium, the Czech Republic, Germany, Spain, the French Republic, Hungary, the Netherlands, the Slovak Republic, the Republic of Finland, Sweden and the UK is also discussed.
- Timothy Kirkhope's report on the draft Council Decision adopting the rules on the confidentiality of Europol information calls for a rejection of the text.
- The rapporteur supports the aim of the Council Decision.
- He would like to keep Europol's remit and scope small and specific.
- PERSON099 calls on the Council to withdraw its proposal on accreditation of forensic laboratory activities.
- Sweden and Spain want to ensure that laboratory activities are accredited by the accreditation body in order to combat crime through closer cooperation between law enforcement authorities in the Member States.
- There are problems regarding the legal basis of this initiative with the entry into force of the Lisbon Treaty.
- The Lisbon Treaty will be in force 7 days and 5 hours from now.
- The Council is in a rush to adopt a series of decisions.
- The rapporteur for Europol and the transfer of personal data and confidential documents to third parties would like to hear from the Council on the issue.
- The European Parliament rejected the Council's proposals concerning Europol's work on the basis of the Treaty of Lisbon.
- The implementation of European internal and security policy has been a task reserved exclusively for the executive.
- The political remoteness of the third pillar of the European Union has made it possible for governments to impose controversial restrictions on the fundamental rights of citizens.
- There is an increasing volume of personal data stored by European security agencies and information systems.
- It is not clear who is allowed to collect, possess, analyse or pass on what data, and the conditions under which they may do so.
- Common minimum standards must be put in place before the European Union signs any further agreements on the exchange of data with third countries.
- Clear data protection standards, a comprehensive proportionality assessment and effective legal protection for citizens are key prerequisites for any further measures.
- The Europol package is made up of four draft decisions that seek to implement the Council Decision of 6 April 2009.
- Member States need to send comprehensive, up-to-date, accurate information to Europol to be able to use its capacity for analysis to the full.
- The scope of application of the draft Council Decision is limited to the processing of data for the purpose of analysis.
- The proposal contains rules and general principles relating to both technical measures and rules for using them.
- The need to collect and process information means that a legal instrument must be created that guarantees full respect of the fundamental rights of European citizens.
- It must also guarantee Europol can carry out the functions assigned to it in full.
- The role of the European Parliament as the House representing citizens is inalienable.
- Parliament should take part in the legislative process on equal footing with the Council.
- The European Crime Prevention Network was set up in 2001 by Council Decision 2001/427/JHA.
- Its objectives were to facilitate cooperation and exchanges of information and experiences at national and European level, collect and analyse relevant information aimed at the exchange of best practices, organise conferences, seminars, meetings and initiatives and provide expertise to the Council and the Commission concerning crime prevention.
- In 2005, the network underwent its first internal structural reform.
- The running of the website was transferred from the European Commission to the United Kingdom.
- In March 2009, an external evaluation was published concerning the functioning of the network.
- The problems brought to light included the lack of suitable resources, an ineffective secretariat, lack of commitment on The proposal to create a crime-prevention network has been rejected by the Swedish Presidency of the European Council.
- The proposal does not envisage any form of cooperation with the European Parliament and the requirement for knowledge of languages has been omitted.
- Civil society, the academic world, the world of experience and experts are being left out of the network.
- Parliament is debating the Treaty of Lisbon and Europol.
- The Commission regrets the fact that Europol is going to become a European agency on 1 January 2010.
- The Council's adoption of this decision was preceded by lengthy negotiations and preparations for its implementation are under way.
- Parliament has rejected the draft Council Decisions governing important aspects of Europol's work.
- Parliament wants a different basis for the framework decision on the accreditation of forensic laboratory activities.
- The Commission is in favour of using accreditation because it encourages a higher quality of work from these laboratories, particularly when it comes to sensitive techniques.
- The Commission is willing to evaluate the implementation and application of this instrument for 1 July 2018.
- The European Union Crime Prevention Network has encountered strategic, political and organisational problems.
- In the short term, the Commission has increased financial support to the network's secretariat.
- Parliament will play its full role in the new strategy against organised crime and crime.
- The Group of the European People's Party is strongly in favour of Europol and Eurodac being put in a strong position in the fight against crime.
- The Commission should take into account the position of the victim as they debate the proposals over the coming months.
- The whole House is asking the Commission to revise the legislative package on Europol.
- The next Commissioner will be asked if she is committed to drawing up legislative proposals on this matter in order for Parliament to legislate.
- The President wants Europol to continue working with Europol and wants it to move forward.
- PERSON108 is concerned about the substance and the procedure of Europol and the pooling of resources to fight organised crime and all kinds of trafficking within the European Union.
- He believes the citizens would misunderstand and misinterpret things if they did not have to wait a few more hours or even days to exercise the colegislative power.
- PERSON109 is the coordinator for the Group of the Greens/European Free Alliance on matters concerning freedom.
- He is concerned about the report on forensic laboratories and decentralisation of research centres in some Member States, such as in Spain.
- He wants to discuss the need for coordination.
- Before the entry into force of the Treaty of Lisbon, the European Parliament has been asked to examine four texts on Europol and the issue of protecting the confidentiality of data passed on by Europol, including to third countries.
- The GUE/NGL Group calls on the Council and the Commission to withdraw these proposals.
- The reports lay down the rules for Europol gathering and exchanging information between EU Member States and third-party nations.
- The most personal information may be collected, including sexual preferences and bank account details.
- This information may even be shared with third parties, Albania, Peru and the Russian Federation.
- Europol officers have immunity from prosecution for anything they do or say in the course of their duties.
- In the recent past, Europol's actions have succeeded in putting an end to human trafficking rings which smuggle in illegal migrants.
- In the future, it would be laudable for Europol to be involved in tackling all kinds of serious international crime.
- The issue of data protection has not been resolved with regard to the plans to grant all authorities unlimited access to data.
